💡 Verb Conjugation (Different Pronouns = Different Verb Forms)
In Spanish, verbs change their endings depending on who is doing the action. For example, "I work" is trabajo, "You work" is trabajas, and "He/She works" is trabaja. Notice how the end of the word changes to match the pronoun!

📚 The 6 Subject Pronouns & Present Indicative Conjugation

In Spanish, there are 6 main categories of subject pronouns. In the present indicative tense (Presente de Indicativo), verbs change their forms (conjugate) into 6 different ways to match these pronouns.

Here is an example using two essential verbs, Ser and Estar. Based on your grammar rules, both mean "to be", but they have different uses (Ser is for permanent attributes and origins, while Estar is for temporary states and locations):

Pronouns (English) Ser (To be) Estar (To be)
Yo (I) soy estoy
Tú (You, singular informal) eres estás
Él / Ella / Usted (He / She / You, singular formal) es está
Nosotros / Nosotras (We) somos estamos
Vosotros / Vosotras (You all, plural informal Spain) sois estáis
Ellos / Ellas / Ustedes (They / You all, plural formal) son están

🔄 Action Verbs: "Yo" vs. "Tú"

Different person = different verb form! In Spanish, the action words (verbs) change shape based on who is doing the action. For regular verbs, the "Yo" (I) form usually ends with the letter -o, while the "Tú" (You) form usually ends with -as or -es.

Also, if it's a reflexive verb (an action you do to yourself, like getting yourself up or washing yourself), the little word in front changes from me to te. Check out how some of our routine verbs change:

Verb (Action) 🙋 Yo (I...) 👉 Tú (You...)
Levantarse (to get up) me levanto te levantas
Desayunar (to have breakfast) desayuno desayunas
Estudiar español (to study Spanish) estudio español estudias español
Almorzar (to eat lunch) almuerzo almuerzas
Cenar (to have dinner) ceno cenas
